1

A town's population increases by \(5\%\) every year. If the population was \(10,000\) in the year \(2000\), which function represents the population \( P \) in the year \( t \)?

\( P = 10,000 \cdot (1.05)^{t-2000} \)

\( P = 10,000 \cdot (0.05)^t \)

\( P = 10,000 \cdot 1.05t \)

\( P = 10,000 \cdot t^{1.05} \)

To solve this, we need to identify the type of growth depicted by the problem. Since the population increases by a percentage each year, this is an example of exponential growth. The formula for exponential growth is \( P(t) = P_0 \cdot (1 + r)^{t} \), where:

  • \( P_0 \) is the initial amount, here it is \(10,000\).
  • \( r \) is the growth rate, which is \(5\%\) or \(0.05\).
  • \( t \) is the number of years since the start time, which is \( t - 2000 \) because we start counting from the year \(2000\).
Therefore, we plug these values into our formula to get \( P = 10,000 \cdot (1.05)^{t-2000} \).
Choice A is correct as it aligns with the exponential growth formula with the correct base and exponent reflecting the start year of \(2000\).
Choice B is incorrect because it uses \(0.05\) as the base of the exponent, which does not represent a \(5\%\) growth from a base population.
Choice C is incorrect because it represents a linear relationship, which is not suitable for percentage growth per year.
Choice D is incorrect because it uses \( t \) as the base and \(1.05\) as the exponent, which does not conform to the exponential growth model.

Substituting these values into the formula, we get \( P = 2000 \cdot (1.05)^{t-2020} \).
Choice A is incorrect because it does not account for the initial time period \((2020)\).
Choice B is incorrect because it uses \(0.05\) as the base of the exponent, which would imply a decrease, not growth.
Choice C is incorrect because it implies linear growth, not exponential.
Choice D is correct because it correctly adjusts for the initial time period, showing the population's growth rate starting from the year \(2020\).
